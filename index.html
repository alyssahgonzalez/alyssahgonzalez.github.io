Alyssah Nicole Gonzalez

Sociologist and applied researcher studying school safety, institutional threat, and criminal justice policy in the wake of mass shootings.

I am a doctoral candidate in Sociology at the University of Texas at Austin, where my research sits at the intersection of education policy, school safety, and criminal justice. My dissertation examines how the 2022 Uvalde school shooting shaped institutional responses across media, school leadership, and law enforcement.

My work draws on mixed methods — content analysis of nearly 300 news articles, in-depth interviews with 60 Texas high school principals, and interviews with 15 school-based law enforcement officers — to understand how safety, threat, and accountability are constructed and enacted at the institutional level.

I am committed to translating rigorous sociological research into actionable policy recommendations for schools, state agencies, and communities navigating the aftermath of gun violence.

Research

My dissertation comprises three interrelated studies on the social and institutional dimensions of school safety following the Uvalde shooting.

Chapter 1 · Content Analysis
Media Framing of Uvalde: Political and Social Discourse Across 295 Articles

A systematic content analysis of 295 news articles from three major outlets examining how the Uvalde shooting was constructed politically, socially, and institutionally in public discourse. This chapter maps the dominant frames, silences, and contested narratives that shaped the policy environment following the shooting.

N = 295 articles · Three news sources · Mixed methods content analysis
Chapter 2 · Principal Interviews
Safety and Institutional Threat: How Texas High School Principals Understand Their Role

Based on 60 in-depth interviews with Texas high school principals, this chapter examines how school leaders conceptualize safety, navigate institutional threat, and balance competing demands from communities, law enforcement, and state policy following Uvalde. Currently in preparation for peer-reviewed publication.

N = 60 interviews · Texas high school principals · Qualitative analysis
Chapter 3 · Law Enforcement Interviews
Training, Accountability, and the School Resource Officer After Uvalde

Drawing on interviews with 15 school-based law enforcement officers in Texas, this chapter investigates how officers understand their training, safety responsibilities, and accountability structures — and how Uvalde reshaped professional identity and institutional expectations in this role.

N = 15 interviews · Texas school-based law enforcement · Qualitative analysis

Policy Brief

Translating dissertation research into recommendations for policymakers, school leaders, and state agencies.

What Texas School Leaders Need After Uvalde

Policy Brief · School Safety · Based on 60 Principal Interviews · University of Texas at Austin

Three years after the Uvalde massacre, Texas school principals continue to navigate significant gaps between state safety mandates and the practical, emotional, and institutional realities of leading schools under threat. This brief presents findings from 60 in-depth interviews with Texas high school principals conducted as part of ongoing dissertation research.

Principals consistently described a safety policy environment that prioritizes compliance and physical infrastructure over the relational, mental health, and training dimensions they identified as most critical. Many reported feeling unsupported by state guidance and unclear about accountability expectations for school-based law enforcement on their campuses.

Key recommendations
1.The Texas Education Agency should develop clearer guidance on principal roles in coordinating with school-based law enforcement, including defined accountability structures post-Uvalde.
2.TCOLE training requirements for school-based law enforcement should be updated to reflect lessons from Uvalde, with mandatory scenario-based preparation for active threat response.
3.State funding for school safety should include dedicated resources for principal professional development in threat assessment, not only physical security infrastructure.
4.Ongoing qualitative research into school leader experiences should be integrated into TEA's school safety review processes to ensure policy reflects on-the-ground realities.
